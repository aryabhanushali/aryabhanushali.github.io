Knowledge-Based AI and the Soup Test

March 15, 2026 • Arya Bhanushali • Knowledge-Based AI, Cognitive Systems, Reasoning

When we talk about knowledge-based AI, we often imagine knowledge graphs, ontologies, and carefully curated rules. But knowledge is only useful if a system can apply it flexibly. To make that concrete, I built a playful artifact called the Soup Test—an interactive page that explores how we categorize something as ordinary as soup. I’m embedding it below and using it as a springboard for why structured knowledge still matters in the age of end-to-end models.

Why pair knowledge bases with interactive concepts?

Large models can imitate reasoning, but knowledge-based AI forces explicit structure: concepts, relations, and constraints. The Soup Test demonstrates how categories are negotiated—temperature, texture, vessel, even cultural context. Encoding those factors into a knowledge graph makes the model’s reasoning inspectable: you can trace why gazpacho counts as soup while a smoothie does not.

Three pillars for modern knowledge-based systems

  • Symbolic backbones: ontologies that capture entities, relations, and rules (e.g., isServedHot, containsBroth, servedInBowl).
  • Neural interfaces: language models to propose candidates, normalize free-form text, and map observations into the symbolic space.
  • Reasoning loops: constraint solvers or graph traversals that explain decisions and expose missing facts.

How I would productionize the Soup Test

  1. Extract the dish attributes shown in the iframe with an LLM, then ground them into a schema (JSON-LD or RDF).
  2. Run rule-based checks ("if it lacks liquid, penalize soup-ness"), but keep uncertainty scores so edge cases can surface.
  3. Store traces of decisions so users can challenge them—"pho is soup because broth + noodles + bowl."

Where this matters

Healthcare triage, financial compliance, and robotics all need systems that can justify their categories, not just guess them. The Soup Test is a lighthearted proxy for those high-stakes domains: if we can’t explain why cereal-with-milk barely misses the soup category, we probably can’t explain why a treatment plan was rejected.

Knowledge-based AI is not about replacing deep models; it is about giving them scaffolding. Pairing structured knowledge with LLM-powered perception yields systems that are both fluent and accountable—and that’s the kind of AI I want to ship.
